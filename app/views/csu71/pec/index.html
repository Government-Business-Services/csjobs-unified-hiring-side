Candidate v3

Candidate v3 UR (Mural board)
{{ govukBackLink({ text: "Back to top", href: "#iterations" }) }}

Design questions

Would 'About this page' guidance help a candidate understand it's purpose?

While the tabs on the hub view have been well received and understood by recruiters, that user group understand the PEC process and can use the info we've supplied effectively. Candidate may not understand what each of the tabs refers to as they don't necessarily understand the PEC process.

What we tried

We added an 'About this page' section to explain what each tab showed, and what they could do on it.

What we found

  • we didn't test this as effectively as we should have. Instead of looking at the message on each tab, the candidates were only really shown the content on the main hub page
  • because of this, we didn't really get any conclusive evidence either way about whether the design was effective.
  • those that did analyse the feature understood it and felt it was valuable guidance, but equally some said they weren't drawn to it visually so would potentially have missed it.

Do candidates want a method of being regularly updated about their PEC progress?

The lack of comms / timescales is a huge issue when a candidate goes through the PEC process. Not knowing how long checks will take leads to unnecessary contact, frustration with timescales, and candidates leaving the process to take other jobs.

What we tried

On the hub page, we added a CTA so that candidates could request updates about their progress.

What we found

  • the option was very well received, with candidates liking the fact they could choose how and how often they'd be contacted
  • most users felt only text and email was needed, and the majority felt only one of those should be required
  • most users questioned how often they'd get updates on the page where they chose text or email. They didn't realise that question was on the following page. It appears they would prefer to set the regularity before choosing text or email, or better still on the same page (although this may go against 'one thing per page' standards)
  • the success banner was universally liked, although it led to chats where we realised there was no option to change duration or method of contact (we had only designed a 'stop updates' option). This needs to be added to a later iteration.

How effective would a check info page be in advising a candidate on progress / timescales?

As above! The lack of comms / timescales is a huge issue when a candidate goes through the PEC process. Not knowing how long checks will take leads to unnecessary contact, frustration with timescales, and candidates leaving the process to take other jobs.

What we tried

For each check, a candidate could click through to an info page which told them (a) what the check was about, (b) how long it usually took and (c) why it might take longer than usual.

What we found

  • the option was well received, with candidates taking the time to read the info and see what it meant for them personally
  • the green 'passed check' page was felt to be too brief for some, and it was suggested it could still show what the check was about
  • the 'not started' version made sense although there was sometimes a feeling that the candidate may have to do something to get the check started
  • most users liked that the 'in progress' page told them they didn't have to do anything
  • the 'action needed' page was well received and the message shown was felt to be appropriately pitched
  • with all versions, the content was key: vagueness or lack of info was not appreciated

Candidate v2 UR (Mural board)
{{ govukBackLink({ text: "Back to top", href: "#iterations" }) }}

Design questions

Would more descriptive status tags help candidates feel 'in the loop'?

Candidates can see status tags on each check, but the info is limited e.g. 'Not started', 'In progress'. We wanted to test whether expanding the description (within the limited space we have) would give a candidate a better idea of how it was progressing, and - importantly - whether the check was currently with an external body such as DBS or a health provider, where we have less control over progress and timescales.

What we tried

We expanded some of the 'In progress' tags to include details such as 'With external health provider', 'With GRS' and 'With Disclosure Scotland'.

What we found

  • Not everyone knew what GRS was, so the content would need to be refined to make it clearer where the check was in this scenario. Some suggested that internal depts didn't need to be mentioned, only if the check had gone outside govt
  • Not everyone knew what the DBS / Disclosure Scotland / AccessNI wording referred to
  • Candidates felt that although useful, the extended tags might still not offer enough info or reassurance. They were, however, keen on an idea we floated during UR: for the PEC system to automatically contact the candidate with updates - by email, text or similar - at regular intervals, such as weekly or fortnight

Could we produce a formal offer journey that would help a candidate understand their offer, what the next steps would be, etc?

After PECs and NEN are complete, the candidate would normally receive a formal offer. This has been criticised for being too impersonal and lacking in the info a candidate needs at this point.

What we tried

We created a formal offer page with content specific to their role.

We also gave the user the option to accept, decline or opt to speak to the hiring manager.

What we found

  • there was some content / links that were considered unnecessary e.g. guidance on grades, list of benefits (especially for internal candidates)
  • some content was missing e.g. terms and conditions / contract
  • users expected contact details for the hiring manager so they could content them, rather than be told to wait for the HR to contact the candidate
  • the confirmation option was well received, although some considered it unnecessary for the 'contact hiring manager' option
  • users expected a grace period where they could change their mind, especially after declining the job
  • some users didn't like the word 'formal' and thought it unnecessary
  • most users expected a phone call to be offered the job, or an email
  • a suggestion was to include a box for reasons for declining, to help VHs assess why campaigns were not successful
  • generic links to your new dept or to search for another job were not always considered welcome

Candidate v1

Candidate v1 UR (Mural board)
{{ govukBackLink({ text: "Back to top", href: "#iterations" }) }}

Design questions

Could we reduce the info a candidate needs to input for PEC checks e.g. pulling info from existing uploads, filtering out unnecessary checks / questions?

Candidates have already had to enter a lot of information before getting to the pre-employment check stage. They then have to enter more / answer multiple questions, sometimes (a) entering data they've already told us, and (b) entering data for checks that may not be necessary for the role.

What we tried

We created a scenario where already submitted info - in this case employment history from an online CV - was pulled into the relevant check, and displayed back to the candidate with the option to submit it as is or amend and submit if necessary.

We also gave a small list of filter questions at the start of the process, which would eliminate unnecessary checks and so save the candidate from time-consuming data entry with no value to the application.

What we found

  • filter questions worked well in terms of reducing unnecessary checks
  • there wasn't much evidence that users understood that their answers led to a bespoke list of checks (but there is some debate about whether this matters?)
  • users were happy to see info pulled in from another source, and most would review it before accepting it as accurate

Could we provide an adequate idea of timescales and check progress to the candidate?

The time checks take - and the lack of visibility on timescales - is a huge issue for recruitment. Candidates often drop out of the process after spending long periods in 'The Black Hole' with no idea of progress.

What we tried

We asked candidates for suggestions about how and when they would want to be kept informed e.g. by logging into their dashboard and seeing progress? Proactive contact by the vacancy holder?

We also asked them about how we could show timescales for checks when we don't know how long they will take.

What we found

  • there were mixed opinions on the best way to keep informed / give timescales
  • some felt candidate hub view was very useful with status tags
  • some wanted specific timescales for checks, but had no definitive idea of how to show timescales on checks outside of govt e.g. health check, overseas check
  • it was suggested that some candidates wouldn't actively log into their account to see progress, so some sort of prompt might be needed e.g. email, text

Would the candidate see comms that required action from them?

One way delay can happen with PECs is if candidates need to perform an action, such as uploading documentation, but don't know they are required to do anything.

What we tried

We prototyped some mock messages from vacancy holders requesting document uploads, to see if the candidate would interact with the message and act accordingly. These were highlighted on the hub view using the Notification Badge component.

What we found

  • the notification badge was obvious and eye-catching
  • candidates said they were drawn to the badge to see what they needed to do
  • they could easily find and read the action message
  • they were confident that they could do the action needed, and were reassured that progress was continuing

Could the 'single view' design enhanced in Recruiter v3 be as useful for a candidate as for other users?

This was our first chance to get candidate feedback on any design that showed how their checks were progressing. This is something they don't currently have - candidates either have to sit and wait for rare and sporadic updates, or actively contact the vacancy holder / checker / recruiting dept for an update.

What we tried

We gave candidates a simplified version of the single view hub tested previously, to see if it was helpful for keeping tabs on progress of checks.

What we found

  • candidates liked the hub and found it useful for getting some idea of progress
  • they liked seeing a history of what had gone on
  • there was a concern that messages sent and received in the service would have to be replicated from their own email conversations if they'd spoken outside of the service
  • some candidates liked the idea of having a way to contact the vacancy holder or checker for an update if nothing had changed for some time

Would direct updates from govt (e.g. by email or text) be useful for a candidate?

To help candidates stay updated on progress, we considered whether we could proactively contact the candidate and tell them what was happening with their pre-employment checks.

What we tried

We asked candidates how useful this would be, how they would want to be contacted, and how frequently we should do this.

We also looked into some of the technicalities and costs of different methods e.g. using Notify to send texts

What we found

  • there were mixed views on whether email or text was better: some preferred text as they felt they'd always read a text, but often wouldn't read an email
  • there were mixed views on how often, and why, we should contact the candidate: some suggested a weekly roundup, others after every check was completed, others at the end of the whole check period
  • Email would be free via Notify, but texts are restricted to 40,000pa. If texts were to be used, then a cost would be incurred or another provider would be needed

Design questions

Would the Right to Work design tested in V5R also work for overseas candidates?

The RtW check is a little more complicated when a candidate is non-UK / Ireland. They often have to provide a 'share code', which the recruiter uses to log into a service on GOV.UK and check / download the candidate's right to work in the UK and Civil Service. Currently this means opening the GOV.UK service, downloading the share code output, uploading it into vX and recording the info manually in the RtW check.

What we tried

We iterated V5R to allow a recruiter to copy and paste the sharecode, open up the GOV.UK service and upload the output directly into the RtW check. The content of the output could also be viewed without opening up the document.

What we found

  • users understood how to perform the overseas check using the new design
  • no users saw any issues with what we had designed, although it was pointed out that different scenarios would need more interaction
  • a 'download' and 'delete' option would be needed for the share code output file uploaded

Design questions

Could we design a useable view where a RtW recruiter could see both an uploaded ID doc and the info needed to compare it with?

At the moment, a recruiter has to download each ID doc that the VH has previously uploaded. They then need to switch between tabs / screens to compare this image with the ID info stored in vX. This is a time-consuming process and makes checking details slower and more open to error.

What we tried

We designed a page with a two-column layout, with ID info on the left (pulled in from sources such as the candidate's PEC form and the VH's verification doc) and a relatively small image on the right corresponding to the left-hand data. If the user hovered over the image, it would use CSS to zoom into the image for better clarity. The image could also be clicked to open it in a new tab or window.

What we found

  • users loved having all the necessary info and images on one page
  • users loved not having to download images before looking at them
  • the zoom function was universally liked and easily used
  • the info shown on the left hand side needed to be everything the user would need to allow them to check the corresponding doc
  • users believed this design would work just as well for more complex EU / RoW cases

Would a uniform naming convention for uploaded ID docs make the RtW checking process easier for recruiters?

ID docs uploaded by VHs can have any file name the VH chooses. This means they often don't even explain what type of doc it is, and recruiters have to download, open and investigate before even starting the checking process.

What we tried

We showed imaginary uploaded docs with a standard file format - application number plus file type e.g. '2987-passport.jpg' - and suggested to users that VH's would be 'forced' to upload docs in a particular filename format, amking it easy to identify what type of document it was.

What we found

  • users were very supportive of having standardised filenames
  • users stated that even if they knew what they were, they would still open the files to check the contents
  • the filename should not be based on training the VH what to call it, but instead should dictate it's own name e.g. application number is known by the system already, VH has to check a box to say it's a driving license = system puts the two together to make a unique filename for this application / candidate
  • one user suggested using the candidate number, but then felt that would not be necessary by this point as they were already on the candidate's page

If we recorded pertinent dates in the timeline, would it save time for recruiters as they'd no longer need to enter dates at regular points in the RtW check?

In vX, recuiters have to enter dates at various points e.g. date check started, check ended.

What we tried

We believed that the system could and should record any relevant dates without forcing a recruiter to manually enter them. We designed a prototype which showed the timeline updating with dates as the user performed certain actions e.g. starting the check, marking the check as Passed or Failed.

What we found

  • the timeline continued to be popular with recruiters
  • users could easily identify that dates were being added to the timeline
  • users felt it would be a timesaver to have this feature rather than entering the dates themselves

Other findings

  • a checkbox to add guidance document(s) to an email requesting additional ID docs was considered very useful
  • a warning panel similar to that used in the duplicate check prototype was universally well received when we used it to flag that there was not enough ID to allow the recuiter to complete the RtW check
  • an easily accessible and prepopulated email requested more docs was successful

    Design questions

    If a Vacancy Holder could automatically check some of the NEN data they'd entered, would that save time / money later in the process?

    At the moment, a Vacancy Holder (VH) enters lots of NEN data which is then sent to GRS to check (at least for Home Office vacancies). GRS have to perform a lot of time-consuming manual checks to see if there are any errors e.g. vacancy is shown as London rated but office address is outside London. These errors have to be flagged to the VH and fixed.

    What we tried

    We showed users a version where the VH had entered all of the NEN data, and there was an option to automatically run checks to find discrepancies. Any discrepancies could be fixed there and then, before passing the corrected data to GRS.

    We adapted the ticket panel component we had tested successfully in V3R to highlight discrepancies, using a red border. Initially we had considered using the standard error summary and error message patterns, but as these were discrepancies rather than data entry validation errors these did not seem the right patterns to use.

    We also continued to use the status tag pattern to both warn of discrepancies and to describe the discrepancy itself.

    What we found

    • users want as much data as possible to be prepopulated from other sources e.g. vacancy details. One user estimated that only about 25% of NEN data is currently prepopulated
    • users liked the automatic checking as it flagged any discrepancies and allowed them to rectify these discrepancies easily. However, it was also noted that VHs would manually check their entries anyway so an automatic check would not be a complete time-saver for them
    • the red panel design worked well and was easily identified as the location of the discrepancy

    Other findings

    • most users did not like the accordion pattern used to divide the NEN form into distinct sections. They preferred to be able to see the whole form in one section, as they were used to scrolling up and down quickly
    • the timeline was not considered very useful for VHs. The info it contained was too detailed for their needs. This could easily be reduced based on their login creds, but there were other issues with the timeline anyway: namely that they could find the same info on the History tab if they needed to, valuable form space was taken up by the timeline and it was leading to a more cluttered look than was necessary
    • the timeline was, however, considered very useful by the GRS user
    • a Save function was considered very important. vX does not allow this currently, so data has to be collated outside of vX until everything is present, and then entered in one go

    Design questions

    Would iterating the 'single view' design into a tabbed layout allow the user easier access to more info / functionality?

    There is a lot of information in PEC and a lot of user needs requiring quick and easy access to info and functionality. It was felt that by 'compartmentalising' the info in a way that users could move quickly between info / function types that it would make PEC progress both quicker and easier.

    What we tried

    We used the tabs component to sectionalise the PEC info into easily accessible chunks. Not only could users navigate around each tab as needed, but some tab content could link to other tabs directly (e.g. the 'Pre-employment checks' tab linked to the 'candidate PEC info' tab when a user asked to view the info entered for a particular check), saving users from bouncing between tabs when looking for info.

    What we found

    • the concept of tabs was familiar to all users
    • tabs were universally welcomed and easily navigated, allowing users access to lots of info quickly
    • there were no recommendations for adding or removing any tabs

    Could we design a way to reuse the result of a previous check, saving time for all users?

    If a candidate has more than one application in, or has recently applied for another govt job, then a particular check may have already been done under that other application. If the result of that existing check (known as a 'duplicate check') could be reused, it would save the candidate re-entering the same information, and the checker having to do the same check again.

    What we tried

    We created a version of the prototype where a duplicate check warning would appear to the checker. This would then give them additional task options - view the duplicate check (to see the result, how long ago it was, which application it was done under), and to ask the vacancy holder of the current application if the check result could be reused rather than done from scratch.

    The duplicate check warning and options were shown in a visually obvious ticket panel component which was adapted to show a red eyecatching edge.

    Permission could be asked by sending a message, and receiving confirmation back. This would then allow the user to mark the decision on the check and prevent duplicate effort.

    What we found

    • one user did not understand what the status tag meant that informed them there was a duplicate check available
    • the display of duplicate check options was easily recognised and visually successful
    • all users saw value in being able to use an existing check, allowing them to save time and effort
    • being able to request permission via the in-service messaging option was universally liked
    • users liked and utilised the history showing messages going back and forth between the checker and the vacancy holder

    Would highlighting new contact from another user be useful?

    Timely and easy communication is seen as a major step towards avoiding the progress 'black hole' where it is difficult for any user to know what is happening with a particular check. We believed that we could help this by not only giving a 'Contact' tab where users could send or receive messages, but also by emphasising when a new message was available. This would allow the reader to see and action the message as soon as possible.

    What we tried

    We used the HMRC and MOJ Notification Badge component to show that a new message was available on the 'Contact' tab, leading the user to the tab where the same component flagged up a new message that could be read.

    What we found

    • one user did not spot the notification badge
    • other users were quickly able to see that there was a new message to read
    • users were able to navigate to the new message and easily read it
    • the ability to reply to the message there and then was welcomed

    Other work - iterations from Recruiter v2

    • complete task journey replicated (EEHC), although no direct access to HMRC's PAYE system was implied to see if a lack of integration would raise issues / concerns
    • comms section expanded to add reply functionality, history entries, view previous messages
    • timeline filter
    • option to allocate task to another user
    • download / print / share timeline
    • central upload area
    • candidate email clickable, phone number added to key details bar

    Design questions

    Would a simple dashboard (showing a list of PECs, their progress, and the option to give each one a status when complete) be good enough for users?

    Firstly, we considered a risky assumption - that a fully fledged system for PECs was not actually needed at all, and instead a simple 'checklist' page which a user could tick when checks were completed would be an improvement on the 'as is' situation.

    What we tried

    We asked users whether a simple checklist would be be beneficial rather than building something more complex.

    What we found

    • this would not offer much improvement (if any) for the main user types - they already had a clunky means of seeing check status in vX
    • it did not solve the problem of users needing multiple systems to action tasks, check progress etc
    • it may have had limited benefit for vacancy holders and candidates if they could access it to see progress of checks

    Can a task list view of PECs work for all users: checkers, managers, vacancy holders, candidates?

    The IxD viewpoint was that a single design (task list and pages hanging off this) could be used for all user types - e.g. checkers, managers, vacancy holders, candidates - rather than design a different set of screens for each user type. The 'single view' design would allow page elements to be shown, hidden or restricted depending on the user that was logged in (with the assumption that login credentials would drive what could and could not be seen / done).

    What we tried

    We iterated the Recruiter V1 task list to create a checker-friendly task list and tested it with checkers and non-checkers, to see if all users could use it successfully (plans were made to test it with candidates in Candidate v1).

    What we found

    • the 'single view' was easily used and widely liked by all users regardless of their role
    • the simple and clean design was a significant improvement over current systems like vX
    • showing or hiding elements could easily be done to cater for relevant user group
    • showing checks that were not needed was worthwhile for reassurance

    Would all workflow allocation types - single task allocated, multiple tasks allocated, entire candidate allocated - be feasible?

    As checkers are one of the biggest PEC user groups, we wanted to challenge how they were currently allocated work (one isolated task at a time) with two other workflow options: giving them a choice from multiple tasks, and allocating an entire candidate - and all of the checks that candidate needed - to the checker.

    What we tried

    We designed three workflow dashboards - a single task, multiple tasks and an entire candidate - and tested all three with users. The designs all used the tabs component to show both available tasks and user work times (to replicate what many are currently used to on the Pre-employment Allocation tool).

    What we found

    • users understood all allocation designs
    • there was no significant preference for any of the workflow suggestions - opinions were quite individual and sometimes depended on experience (e.g. had worked a candidate view before) or motivational (e.g. wanted to see a list of tasks going down as it was worked)
    • we should consider whether giving users a choice when signing in would cover all needs

    Would access to comms - and the ability to reply to them - be useful?

    A significant issue for users like checkers is that they have to use multiple systems to send and receive comms with candidates, vacancy holders etc. We wanted to see if building in some basic comms info would be beneficial.

    What we tried

    We added fictional emails into the timeline, with the option to reply to that email from the timeline.

    What we found

    • users often spotted the email in the timeline
    • they liked the opportunity to reply there and then
    • showing a full email in the timeline could be overkill, especially if there was a long email conversation leading to multiple entries in the timeline

    Other work - iterations from Recruiter V1

    • expanding timeline on task list page was dropped, as the need for subsequent task options meant jumping to another page would usually be necessary, defeating the purpose of quickly showing the timeline
    • timeline content was updated to try and reflect more real-world scenarios for PECs
    • key details bar was improved - reduced in size, improved content e.g. candidate's email address
    • status tag content was improved to show outcome of check e.g. PASSED, RISK ASSESSMENT NEEDED
    • ability to change task status was added

    Initial design was agreed with Service Designer (Oceanne) and limited to:

    1. sign in page
    2. 2FA page
    3. user dashboard
    4. list of PECs

    Was agreed to reuse existing design of pages 1-3 from existing campaign / sift prototype, as these had been extensively tested already. The designs were not refined for PEC, as they were simply to drive conversation about what a user would want to see on each page.

    Page 4 (list of PECs) utilised the existing task list pattern, as (a) it was a standard pattern and (b) it had already been used and tested in campaign / sift prototype.

    The DWP Key Details Bar - already tested on agent-facing services - was added to give prominent info on the candidate's details at all times.

    The user was given two designs for viewing historical info on each check. Both designs used the same timeline component to display the info, as this was a tested pattern and the IxD (Jonathan) had also worked on DWP services where this had been extensively tested (e.g. Winter Fuel Payments, Get your State Pension). The timeline was populated with dummy content to drive requirements from the user during testing.

    The first design used the Reveal pattern to show the timeline when the task name was clicked (the reveal was amended to hide the arrow icon to retain the task list look and feel).

    The second design took the user to a new page which displayed the same timeline info.

    At all relevant points, the tag component was used to indicate status of the check at that point.
